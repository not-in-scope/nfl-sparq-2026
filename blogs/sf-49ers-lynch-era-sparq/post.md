# Nine Drafts, One Standard: The John Lynch Era by the Numbers

*May 1, 2026*

*John Lynch took over as 49ers GM with zero front-office experience. Nine years later, two Super Bowl appearances. Here's what the combine data says about how he built it.*

---

John Lynch had never worked in an NFL front office when Kyle Shanahan called him in January 2017. He'd just retired from the Fox Sports broadcast booth. The 49ers had gone 2-14 the previous year. The roster was bare.

What happened next — two Super Bowl appearances, three NFC Championship games, and a consistent identity built around athletic football players — is one of the more interesting front-office stories of the modern NFL. We have SPARQ z-scores for every Lynch draft class. Here's what they say.

---

## The Year-by-Year Record

![SF 49ers SPARQ draft grades, Lynch/Shanahan era 2017-2026](sf-lynch-era-sparq.png)

Eight of ten Lynch-era draft classes have averaged a positive SPARQ z-score. The only exception is 2020 at -0.46, and that class had only five picks, with Javon Kinlaw (R1, no combine data due to COVID cancellation) pulling down the average. The trend line runs clearly upward: the three most recent draftable classes (2024, 2025, 2026) are the most athletically tested in Lynch's tenure.

The overall Lynch-era SPARQ average, weighted equally across all classes: **+0.27**. That's a full quarter of a standard deviation above the league mean, sustained across a decade of drafts.

---

## The Foundation Years: 2017-2018

Lynch's first two classes — +0.48 and +0.39 — were the quiet bricklaying of what the 49ers would become.

**2017** produced George Kittle in the fifth round. Kittle's SPARQ z-score is +0.93, driven by his explosion and agility numbers that were exceptional for a tight end. Lynch and Shanahan saw a player whose testing profile matched what they needed in their wide zone scheme. Most teams passed on him 145 times. The 49ers also took Solomon Thomas third overall — z=+2.32, an elite tester who ultimately didn't produce as an NFL pass rusher. That's the tension that runs through this entire analysis: athleticism is necessary but not sufficient. Thomas had the testing profile. He didn't have the right position or the right usage.

**2018** was shorter on volume — Jimmy Garoppolo tore his ACL in week three and the season collapsed to 4-12 — but the draft produced Fred Warner in the third round. Warner's z-score is +1.00. He's been to the Pro Bowl four times and is widely considered one of the two or three best linebackers in the NFL. You don't find Warner without testing well. His athleticism is what made him viable as a three-down linebacker in a complex scheme.

The 2017 and 2018 classes are the foundation of every good 49ers team since. Kittle and Warner are still on the roster.

---

## The Anomaly Years: 2019-2023

This is where the data gets more complicated — and more interesting.

**2019's class averaged +0.09**. Nick Bosa went second overall with a z-score of +1.01 — the class's standout tester, a generational pass rusher. Deebo Samuel went 36th overall at +0.36. Dre Greenlaw fell to the fifth round at +0.23. On its face, this looks like a mediocre SPARQ class. It produced a 13-3 season and a Super Bowl appearance.

The lesson here isn't that the 2019 class was underrated athletically — it's that the top of the class was elite, and the average was pulled down by late-round developmental picks. Bosa and Samuel are both in the top 20% of athletic profiles at their positions. The team's identity was built on those two players for the next five years.

**2020's class is the one negative data point**: -0.46 average, only four scored picks, a COVID year that wiped out the combine. Brandon Aiyuk came out at +0.23 from Arizona State. Javon Kinlaw, the other first-round pick, had no combine data. The season was 6-10, gutted by injuries. Whether the testing profiles caused this or the injuries did is impossible to separate.

**2021, 2022, and 2023** are the three quiet years: +0.18, +0.08, and +0.13. SF drafted between adequate and below-average athletically by our measure, while going to the NFC Championship twice and the Super Bowl once. How?

Two reasons. First, by 2021, the foundation players from 2017-2019 were in their prime: Kittle, Warner, Bosa, Samuel, Aiyuk. A team doesn't need elite athletes in every class when it's already built. Second — and this is the part the SPARQ data will never fully capture — **Brock Purdy happened**.

---

## The Purdy Exception

Brock Purdy was the 262nd pick of the 2022 draft. Mr. Irrelevant. His SPARQ z-score is -0.05. By our primary metric, he tests as an average NFL athlete at best, below average among quarterbacks who make it to the combine.

He has been the best quarterback in the NFL for stretches of the last three seasons.

This is not a contradiction of the SPARQ thesis. It's a limitation of it. SPARQ measures the physical attributes that predict NFL success across most positions — speed, explosion, agility, strength. For quarterbacks, the inputs that matter most are processing speed, pocket management, and accuracy under pressure. None of those are captured by the combine. The historical data bears this out: SPARQ correlation with QB success is dramatically lower than for skill positions and defensive line.

Lynch found an outlier. The data can't explain it. The argument isn't that SPARQ explains everything — it's that it explains more than people think, and that it's particularly useful for non-quarterback positions.

---

## The Reset: 2024-2026

The 49ers went 6-11 in 2024. Injuries to Deebo Samuel, Brandon Aiyuk, and Nick Bosa, plus a difficult stretch from Brock Purdy, collapsed a team that was a point from winning Super Bowl LVIII the year before.

The response in the draft room has been unmistakable.

**2024** averaged +0.64 — the best Lynch class in seven years. Ricky Pearsall (R1, +0.97), Isaac Guerendo (R4, +1.58), and Malik Mustapha (R4, +1.37) give that class three players in the top quartile of athletic profiles at their positions. All eight scored picks have data. Nothing is hidden.

**2025** came in at +0.60, led by Nick Martin (R3, +1.48) and CJ West (R4, +1.30). Mykel Williams, the R1 edge rusher, had no combine data due to injury — if his pro-day numbers hold, the average climbs further.

**2026 is the highest-SPARQ class Lynch has ever assembled: +0.82**. Eight picks, eight with data — zero missing. Romello Height at +1.20, Kaelon Black at +1.41, Gracen Halton at +1.18. The 49ers drafted as if they're done rebuilding and ready to load up.

---

## What the Pattern Tells You

The Lynch era doesn't tell a simple story of SPARQ = wins. It's more nuanced than that.

What it shows: Lynch has been consistently willing to draft athletic players at every position except quarterback, where the metric doesn't apply. The foundation players — Kittle, Warner, Bosa — are all legitimate athletic standouts who tested in the top 20-30% at their positions. The draft busts — Thomas, Lance — were either poor fits for the scheme or undone by injury and off-field issues, not by lack of athleticism. The one great outlier (Purdy) is at the one position where SPARQ doesn't predict outcomes.

The 2024-2026 run of +0.64, +0.60, and +0.82 classes suggests Lynch is building again the way he built the 2019-era team: athletically, with depth, without cutting corners. The 2017 and 2018 classes turned into a Super Bowl run by year three. If the pattern holds, 2027 and 2028 will be worth watching.

The historical validation we ran on the 2021 and 2022 classes showed an r=0.42 correlation between team SPARQ average at draft time and starter rate three years later. San Francisco, built on this standard for nine years, is the clearest long-form case study in why that number matters.
