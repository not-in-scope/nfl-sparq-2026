Nine Drafts, One Standard: The John Lynch Era by the Numbers

John Lynch took over as 49ers GM with zero front-office experience. Nine years later, two Super Bowl appearances. Here's what the combine data says about how he built it.

John Lynch had never worked in an NFL front office when Kyle Shanahan called him in January 2017. He'd just retired from the Fox Sports broadcast booth. The 49ers had gone 2-14 the previous year. The roster was bare.

What happened next — two Super Bowl appearances, three NFC Championship games, and a consistent identity built around athletic football players — is one of the more interesting front-office stories of the modern NFL. We have SPARQ z-scores for every Lynch draft class. Here's what they say.

The Year-by-Year Record

Average SPARQ z-score per draft class, scored picks only. Season record and playoff result shown below each bar. SB = Super Bowl appearance; NFCCG = NFC Championship Game.

Eight of ten Lynch-era draft classes have averaged a positive SPARQ z-score. The only exception is 2020 at -0.46, a COVID year with no combine and only five picks — Javon Kinlaw's first-round slot had no data at all. The trend runs clearly upward: the three most recent classes (2024, 2025, 2026) are the most athletically tested of Lynch's tenure.

The overall Lynch-era SPARQ average, weighted equally across all classes: +0.27. A full quarter of a standard deviation above the league mean, sustained across a decade of drafts.

The Foundation Years: 2017-2018

Lynch's first two classes — +0.48 and +0.39 — were the quiet bricklaying of what the 49ers would become.

2017 produced George Kittle in the fifth round. Kittle's SPARQ z-score is +0.93, driven by explosion and agility numbers that were exceptional for a tight end. Lynch and Shanahan saw a player whose testing profile matched what their wide zone scheme needed. Most teams passed on him 145 times. The 49ers also took Solomon Thomas third overall — z=+2.32, an elite tester who didn't produce as an NFL pass rusher. That tension runs through this entire analysis: athleticism is necessary but not sufficient. Thomas had the profile. He didn't have the right position or the right usage.

2018 was short on volume — Jimmy Garoppolo tore his ACL in week three and the season collapsed to 4-12 — but the draft produced Fred Warner in the third round. Warner's z-score is +1.00. Four Pro Bowls. One of the two or three best linebackers in the league. You don't find Warner without testing well. His athleticism is what made him viable as a three-down linebacker in a complex scheme.

The 2017 and 2018 classes are the foundation of every good 49ers team since. Kittle and Warner are still on the roster.

The Anomaly Years: 2019-2023

This is where the data gets more complicated — and more interesting.

2019's class averaged +0.09. Nick Bosa went second overall with a z-score of +1.01 — the class's standout tester, a generational pass rusher. Deebo Samuel went 36th overall at +0.36. Dre Greenlaw fell to the fifth round at +0.23. On its face, this looks like a mediocre SPARQ class. It produced a 13-3 season and a Super Bowl appearance. The lesson: the average was pulled down by late-round picks, but the top of the class was elite. Bosa and Samuel are both in the top 20% of athletic profiles at their positions. The team's identity was built on those two players for five years.

2020 is the one negative data point at -0.46. Brandon Aiyuk came out at +0.23. Javon Kinlaw had no combine due to COVID. The season was 6-10, gutted by injuries. Whether the testing profiles caused this or the injuries did is impossible to separate.

2021, 2022, and 2023 averaged +0.18, +0.08, and +0.13 respectively — three quiet years in which SF went to the NFC Championship twice and the Super Bowl once. How? By 2021, the foundation players from 2017-2019 were in their prime: Kittle, Warner, Bosa, Samuel, Aiyuk. A team doesn't need elite athletes in every class when it's already built. And then Brock Purdy happened.

The Purdy Exception

Brock Purdy was the 262nd pick of the 2022 draft. Mr. Irrelevant. His SPARQ z-score is -0.05. By our primary metric, he tests as below average among quarterbacks who make it to the combine.

He has been the best quarterback in the NFL for stretches of the last three seasons.

This is not a contradiction of the SPARQ thesis — it's a limitation of it. SPARQ measures physical attributes that predict NFL success across most positions: speed, explosion, agility, strength. For quarterbacks, what matters most is processing speed, pocket management, and accuracy under pressure. None of those are captured by the combine. The historical SPARQ correlation with QB success is dramatically lower than for skill positions and defensive line.

Lynch found an outlier. The data can't explain it. The argument isn't that SPARQ explains everything — it's that it explains more than people think, and it's particularly useful for every position that isn't quarterback.

The Reset: 2024-2026

The 49ers went 6-11 in 2024. Injuries to Samuel, Aiyuk, and Bosa, plus a difficult year from Purdy, collapsed a team that was a point from winning Super Bowl LVIII.

The response in the draft room has been unmistakable.

2024 averaged +0.64 — the best Lynch class in seven years. Ricky Pearsall (R1, +0.97), Isaac Guerendo (R4, +1.58), and Malik Mustapha (R4, +1.37) give that class three players in the top quartile of athletic profiles at their positions. All eight scored picks have data.

2025 came in at +0.60, led by Nick Martin (R3, +1.48) and CJ West (R4, +1.30). Mykel Williams, the R1 edge rusher, had no combine data — if his pro-day numbers hold, the class average climbs further.

2026 is the highest-SPARQ class Lynch has ever assembled: +0.82. Eight picks, eight with data — zero missing. Romello Height at +1.20, Kaelon Black at +1.41, Gracen Halton at +1.18. The 49ers drafted as if they're done rebuilding and ready to load up.

What the Pattern Tells You

The Lynch era doesn't tell a simple story of SPARQ = wins. It's more nuanced than that.

What it shows: Lynch has been consistently willing to draft athletic players at every position except quarterback, where the metric doesn't apply. The foundation players — Kittle, Warner, Bosa — are all legitimate athletic standouts who tested in the top 20-30% at their positions. The draft busts — Thomas, Lance — were either poor fits for the scheme or undone by injury, not lack of athleticism. The one great outlier (Purdy) is at the one position where SPARQ doesn't predict outcomes.

The 2024-2026 run of +0.64, +0.60, and +0.82 classes suggests Lynch is building again the way he built the 2019-era team: athletically, with depth, without cutting corners. The 2017 and 2018 classes turned into a Super Bowl run by year three. The historical validation we ran on the 2021-2022 classes showed an r=0.42 correlation between team SPARQ average at draft time and starter rate three years later.

San Francisco, built on this standard for nine years, is the clearest long-form case study in why that number matters.
